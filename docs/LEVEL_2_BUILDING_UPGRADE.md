# Level 2 Building Upgrade Path

IX-Bayanihan-HydroGrid is designed to begin at lot scale.

The primary deployment model is the **HydroGrid Lot** because it is the fastest realistic path to field evidence, partner review, controlled testing, and public-works handoff.

The architecture is not limited to lot scale.

This document defines the **Level 2 HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Building**: a future higher-capacity upgrade path for partners that need more flood support than a modular lot can provide.

A Level 2 building is not the first recommended deployment. It is the permanent infrastructure path that may become appropriate after lot-scale evidence demonstrates value.

## Core Concept

A HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Building is a purpose-built vertical flood-control utility node.

It is not an ordinary building with flood equipment added afterward.

It is designed around flood response from the beginning, with lower levels dedicated to water interface and pump support, protected upper levels dedicated to power, controls, maintenance, communications, and operations.

The building-scale concept exists for situations where a city, province, public-works agency, donor, utility, or infrastructure partner needs a more permanent and higher-capacity form of the HydroGrid architecture.

## Why a Building Version Exists

A lot-scale HydroGrid installation can support selected chokepoints.

A building-scale HydroGrid installation can support larger or more complex flood-control needs, including:

- higher pump capacity;
- larger battery reserves;
- protected equipment above flood level;
- maintenance staging;
- spare-parts storage;
- local command operations;
- stronger communications;
- support for multiple nearby flood chokepoints;
- integration with existing pump stations;
- longer-duration emergency operation;
- permanent public-works presence;
- deeper event logging and review.

The building path matters because some flood-prone areas may eventually need more than a modular lot.

The lot proves the concept. The building expands the capacity.

## Deployment Role

A Level 2 HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Building may serve as:

- a pump-support facility;
- a flood-sensor operations node;
- a local emergency power node;
- a maintenance staging facility;
- a water-treatment support node;
- a flood-event blackbox logging center;
- a communications gateway;
- a public-works coordination site;
- a multi-lot cluster control point;
- a permanent upgrade for a high-risk drainage basin.

The specific role depends on site conditions and public-works requirements.

## What the Building Could Do

A properly sited and engineered HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Building could:

- support higher flow rates than a single lot-scale pilot;
- operate multiple pump lines or pump clusters;
- keep sensitive electronics above flood level;
- maintain power to sensors, pumps, lights, and communications during outages;
- provide protected maintenance access;
- store spare pumps, screens, valves, sensors, hoses, and tools;
- log flood events across multiple nodes;
- classify local flood failure modes using sensor fusion;
- support evacuation-center access, emergency road access, pump-station approaches, or drainage chokepoints;
- serve as a hardened operations point during rainy-season events.

This does not mean the building makes the surrounding area flood-proof.

It means the building can provide enough permanent capacity and operational support to matter at selected flood basins or high-value chokepoints.

## What the Building Cannot Do

A HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Building cannot:

- stop typhoons;
- stop storm surge;
- stop river overflow;
- eliminate citywide flooding;
- replace major drainage infrastructure;
- replace basin-scale hydrology planning;
- work without a valid discharge path;
- work without maintenance crews;
- work without local authority approval;
- guarantee life safety;
- guarantee property protection;
- move water into a receiving path that has no capacity;
- solve flood problems caused by downstream blockage, tide lock, or basin overflow by itself.

The building is a powerful support node, not a miracle solution.

## Building Design Principle

The building should be organized vertically by flood risk and system criticality.

Lower levels interact with water.

Upper levels protect power, controls, maintenance resources, and command functions.

A conceptual floor stack may look like this:

```
Roof:
  Solar support, weather station, communications mast, beacon, inspection access

Upper operations levels:
  Command room, dashboard, radio gateway, event logging, public-works interface

Maintenance levels:
  spare pumps, valves, hose sections, debris tools, sensor replacements, workbench

Power and control levels:
  protected batteries, power electronics, controllers, communications core, safe shutdown systems

Water-support levels:
  water-treatment support, utility-water handling, emergency service systems where approved

Lower pump levels:
  pump gallery, motor controls, valves, manifolds, flow meters, pressure sensors

Ground/flood interface:
  intake apron, debris screen, trash rack, sump, access gates, discharge routing
```
The exact layout must be designed by qualified professionals for the site.

This repository does not provide construction-ready floor plans.

Ground and Flood Interface

The lowest portion of the building is the flood interface.

Potential functions include:

intake apron;
curb or canal intake channel;
coarse trash rack;
sloped screen;
removable debris basket;
sediment trap;
pump sump;
access gates;
service walkway;
flood-safe barriers;
backflow isolation;
inspection ports;
safe crew access.

This level is the most exposed to debris, contaminated water, impact, sediment, and flood loads.

It must be designed for safe maintenance and failure isolation.

Pump Gallery

The pump gallery is where the building-scale system becomes materially different from a lot.

Potential functions include:

multiple dirty-water pumps;
pump manifolds;
isolation valves;
check valves;
flow meters;
pressure sensors;
pump-current monitoring;
vibration monitoring;
cavitation detection;
discharge-routing controls;
manual override;
safe shutdown controls;
service hoist or pump-removal access.

A building-scale pump gallery can support larger pump capacity, better redundancy, easier maintenance, and more permanent instrumentation than a small lot.

The pump gallery must be hydraulically designed for the site.

Discharge System

The discharge system determines whether the building can actually help.

A building-scale pump system needs a reviewed receiving path.

Possible receiving paths include:

storm drain;
canal;
river;
approved outfall;
retention basin;
detention basin;
pump-station intake;
transfer main;
controlled storage;
approved temporary discharge route.

Every discharge path must be reviewed for:

receiving capacity;
backflow risk;
downstream flooding risk;
contamination risk;
legal approval;
environmental impact;
maintenance access;
tide or surge influence where relevant.

If the receiving path cannot accept water, the building cannot solve the local flood problem.

Power Systems

A building-scale system can support larger and more protected power systems than a lot.

Potential power components include:

grid service;
transfer switch;
battery bank;
battery management system;
protected DC bus;
inverters;

The power system must be professionally designed and reviewed.

Energy systems in wet flood environments create serious hazards if installed incorrectly.

Battery Placement

Battery systems should be located above expected flood levels and protected from:

water intrusion;
impact;
overheating;
unauthorized access;
corrosive air;
poor ventilation;
electrical faults;
fire propagation;
maintenance hazards.

Battery chemistry, enclosure design, thermal management, fire-safety requirements, and ventilation must be reviewed by qualified professionals.

This repository does not approve any battery installation.

Fuel Cell or Hydrogen Option

A Level 2 building may include a fuel-cell or hydrogen-backed backup energy option only if the partner has the capability to design, permit, install, inspect, and maintain it safely.

Hydrogen, if used, is backup energy.

It is not a floodwater-disposal method.

Hydrogen systems require:

certified equipment;
code-compliant storage;
ventilation;
leak detection;
flame and ignition control;
emergency shutdown;
safe separation;
trained maintenance;
fire-safety review;
local approval.

Hydrogen storage should not be casually placed inside occupied or uncontrolled building spaces.

A building-scale HydroGrid system can be designed to accept hydrogen-backed power in the future without requiring hydrogen in the first implementation.

Water-Treatment Support

A Level 2 building may include limited water-treatment support, depending on local needs and approvals.

Possible functions include:

utility-water filtration for equipment washdown;
sediment settling for maintenance handling;
emergency non-potable water processing;
controlled sample collection;
water-quality monitoring;
pretreatment for approved technical subsystems;
safe handling of contaminated floodwater.

The building should not claim to create potable water unless a qualified water-treatment design, testing plan, and regulatory approval are included.

Floodwater may be contaminated with sewage, chemicals, sediment, hydrocarbons, biological hazards, metals, and debris.

Water-treatment functions require professional review.

Littoral Sentinel Integration

The building can host the HydroGrid Littoral Sentinel Layer where relevant.

This may support:

salinity sensing for backflow detection;
water-level comparison across outfalls;
wave or surge sensing near coastal zones;
hydrophone-style acoustic sensing for pump cavitation, turbulence, or debris strikes;
vibration-based pump-health diagnostics;
turbidity sensing;
floating sentinel nodes;
rugged canal or coastal sensors;
confidence-gated flood classification.

This layer is civilian and water-focused.

It does not include defense, targeting, disabling, stealth, electronic attack, or weapon behavior.

Sensor Fusion and Flood Classification

A building-scale node can classify flood states across several nearby sensors and lots.

Candidate flood states include:

normal;
rainfall active;
water rising;
flood threshold reached;
intake clog suspected;
outlet blockage suspected;
pump active with verified flow;
pump active with poor flow;
pump cavitation suspected;
pump dry-run suspected;
backflow suspected;
salinity increase detected;
downstream receiving path restricted;
power degraded;
communications degraded;
maintenance required;
sensor fault suspected.

Classifications should be evidence-based and confidence-gated.

No single sensor should be allowed to declare a major failure without supporting evidence.

Capacity Thinking

The useful capacity of a HydroGrid building depends on:

pump size;
number of pumps;
pump head;
intake design;
debris load;
discharge capacity;
receiving water level;
power capacity;
maintenance crew response;
control logic;
operating rules.

A building with large pumps but poor discharge cannot help.

A building with good sensors but no maintenance cannot sustain performance.

A building with strong batteries but undersized pumps may preserve awareness but not move enough water.

Capacity must be evaluated as a complete system.

How It Affects Flooding

A network of properly sited HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Buildings could materially reduce flood pain at selected priority areas.

Potential effects include:

faster drainage at selected chokepoints;
improved emergency access;
longer pump operation during power instability;
better debris response;
stronger public-works visibility;
better post-event accountability;
reduced flood duration in serviced areas;
improved resilience around evacuation centers, schools, clinics, and pump-station approaches.

The building-scale system would matter most where local flooding is driven by pump limits, clogged intakes, poor visibility, power fragility, or maintenance delay.

It would matter less where flooding is driven by basin-scale overflow, storm surge beyond defenses, or receiving waters that cannot accept discharge.

Why It Could Matter

A building-scale node matters because flood response is not only about water volume.

Flood response also depends on:

knowing what is failing;
keeping power available;
keeping communications available;
maintaining pumps;
clearing debris;
staging crews;
preserving access;
documenting what happened;
coordinating multiple sites.

A HydroGrid building gives those functions a protected physical base.

That is why the building path is included.

Strategic Placement

HydroGrid buildings should be placed by failure geometry, not evenly.

Candidate placement categories include:

pump-station support;
underpass support;
evacuation-center access;
hospital or clinic access;
school shelter access;
canal or trash-choke support;
estuary backflow support;
coastal surge interface;
high-risk drainage basin support;
multi-lot cluster command point.

The best site is not always the largest flood area.

The best site is where the building can protect a high-value chokepoint with a valid discharge path and maintainable equipment.

Build Sequence

A responsible building upgrade path should follow this sequence:
```
1. HydroGrid Lot concept review
2. Single-site feasibility review
3. Controlled wet test
4. Single HydroGrid Lot pilot
5. Pilot data review
6. Multi-lot cluster if justified
7. Basin-level hydraulic study
8. Level 2 building feasibility report
9. Public-works and permitting review
10. Professional engineering design
11. Procurement and construction planning
12. Commissioning test
13. Rainy-season operational validation
14. Post-event performance review
```

The building should not be the first step unless a qualified partner already has the site study and infrastructure program underway.

Readiness Criteria

A partner may be ready to explore a Level 2 building if:

one or more flood chokepoints are already documented;
discharge paths are known;
pump-station or drainage data exists;
public-works authority is engaged;
maintenance ownership is possible;
funding path exists;
engineering review is available;
lot-scale or related pilot evidence supports expansion;
safety and permitting pathways are understood.

A partner is not ready if:

the site is unknown;
discharge path is unknown;
maintenance owner is unknown;
public authority is not engaged;
funding is only conceptual;
no engineering review is planned;
the building is being described as flood-proofing.

Summary

The Level 2 HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Building is the project’s high-capacity future path.

It exists so partners understand that the architecture is not capped at lot scale.

The lot is the practical first deployment.

The building is the permanent municipal upgrade path.

A building-scale system could matter because it can combine higher pumping capacity, protected power, maintenance staging, command operations, communications, sensor fusion, and event logging in one hardened utility node.

It still cannot make a city flood-proof.

It can, if properly sited and professionally engineered, make selected flood basins and priority chokepoints less blind, less power-fragile, easier to maintain, and faster to recover.
