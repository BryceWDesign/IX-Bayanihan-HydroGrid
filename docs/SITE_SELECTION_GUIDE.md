# Site Selection Guide

IX-Bayanihan-HydroGrid should be deployed only where the flood problem is specific, observable, maintainable, and hydraulically reviewable.

The system is not intended to be placed randomly.

A HydroGrid Lot should be placed where it can improve visibility, support pumping, protect priority access, guide maintenance, or preserve local operations during flood events.

The best site is not always the deepest flood location.

The best site is the location where a bounded intervention can be measured, maintained, reviewed, and connected to a valid discharge path.

## Purpose

This guide helps partners identify candidate locations for a HydroGrid Lot or future HydroGrid Cluster.

It is intended to support early planning, not replace professional civil, hydraulic, environmental, electrical, or public-works review.

No site should proceed to deployment based only on this guide.

## Core Site Rule

A HydroGrid Lot requires three things:

```
1. A real flood pain point
2. A valid way to move, monitor, or manage water
3. A responsible party that can maintain the system
```

If any of these are missing, the site is not ready.

Site Selection Priorities

A strong HydroGrid site usually has at least one of the following:

repeated flood history;
known drainage chokepoint;
critical access function;
public-works relevance;
available maintenance access;
known discharge path;
ability to measure water movement;
ability to compare before and after conditions;
local authority interest;
manageable safety boundary;
realistic power and communications options.

A weak site usually has:

no clear flood source;
no valid discharge path;
no maintenance owner;
unsafe public exposure;
uncertain land ownership;
inaccessible equipment location;
uncontrolled downstream risk;
no way to measure improvement;
unrealistic performance expectations.
Candidate Site Types
Underpasses and Road Sumps

Underpasses and road sumps can be strong candidates because they collect water, disrupt movement, and often create immediate public-safety issues.

Potential benefits:

faster water-level detection;
pump-support visibility;
vehicle-risk warning;
flow verification;
local lighting or beacon support;
maintenance alerting.

Required checks:

where the water enters;
where the water can be discharged;
whether existing drains are blocked or undersized;
whether pumping could worsen another location;
whether equipment can be protected from vehicles;
whether crews can safely access the site.
Evacuation-Center Access Routes

Access routes to schools, gyms, barangay halls, clinics, or public buildings used during disasters can be high-value sites.

Potential benefits:

preserving access longer;
reducing post-rain flood duration;
warning operators when water rises;
powering local lights or communications;
documenting repeated access failures.

Required checks:

whether the route has a repeat flood history;
whether a discharge path exists;
whether flood depth can be measured safely;
whether equipment can be placed without blocking evacuation;
who maintains the installation.
Hospital, Clinic, and Emergency Access Roads

Medical access routes may justify priority because delays can create serious consequences.

Potential benefits:

early warning;
pump support at known low points;
maintenance prioritization;
local backup power for flood-status equipment;
evidence for future infrastructure upgrades.

Required checks:

emergency vehicle clearance;
road safety;
discharge routing;
public authority approval;
continuous maintenance plan;
electrical safety in wet areas.
Pump-Station Approaches

Sites near existing pumping stations may be strong candidates because HydroGrid can support visibility, debris detection, and backup awareness around existing infrastructure.

Potential benefits:

monitoring inflow;
detecting trash-rack problems;
identifying pump-adjacent flooding;
preserving local sensor power;
supporting maintenance crews;
logging conditions before water reaches the main station.

Required checks:

station owner coordination;
pump-station operating rules;
intake and outlet conditions;
backflow behavior;
maintenance access;
data-sharing expectations;
electrical and safety boundaries.
Canal and Creek Chokepoints

Canal edges, creek crossings, and drainage corridors can be useful locations when debris or constriction repeatedly worsens flooding.

Potential benefits:

water-level monitoring;
debris accumulation alerts;
salinity or backflow sensing where relevant;
maintenance prioritization;
event logging for public-works review.

Required checks:

waterway ownership or authority;
safe installation method;
debris profile;
vandalism risk;
sensor protection;
crew access;
downstream impact.
Drainage Inlets and Curb Low Points

Curb inlets and low street points may be useful for small HydroGrid Lots or pump-pod clusters.

Potential benefits:

detecting local ponding;
verifying drain performance;
identifying inlet clogging;
supporting small pump assistance;
logging repeated nuisance flooding.

Required checks:

traffic safety;
pedestrian safety;
debris load;
exact discharge path;
pump head;
hose or pipe routing;
public-space approval.
Coastal Outfalls and Estuary Interfaces

Coastal and estuary sites may require the Littoral Sentinel Layer.

Potential benefits:

salinity-based backflow detection;
tide or surge awareness;
outfall status monitoring;
canal-to-coast flood-state comparison;
coastal warning support;
rugged waterborne sensing.

Required checks:

tide conditions;
storm surge exposure;
seawater corrosion;
marine fouling;
outfall ownership;
discharge rules;
sensor anchoring;
coastal safety review.
Retention and Detention Basin Edges

Basin edges may be useful for floating solar, water-level monitoring, pump support, or controlled storage.

Potential benefits:

basin-level monitoring;
floating solar support;
pump staging;
controlled discharge measurement;
event logging;
energy support for nearby flood nodes.

Required checks:

basin ownership;
water-level operating rules;
safe access;
wildlife or environmental constraints;
floating equipment anchoring;
public access control;
maintenance route.
Site Selection Questions

Before choosing a site, answer the following.

Flood Problem
What floods here?
How often does it flood?
What depth does it reach?
How long does water remain?
What gets disrupted?
Who is affected?
Is the problem repeated or rare?
Is there a known flood history?
Is the flood mainly rainfall, drainage failure, backflow, river overflow, coastal surge, or combined?
Water Source
Where does the water come from?
Does it arrive from road runoff?
Does it arrive from an upstream drain?
Does it backflow from a canal or outfall?
Does it come from river overflow?
Does it come from tidal or coastal influence?
Does debris arrive with the water?
Does sediment accumulate?
Water Destination
Where can water legally and safely go?
Is there an existing drain?
Is there a canal?
Is there a culvert?
Is there a retention basin?
Is there a pump station?
Is there an approved outfall?
Is there temporary storage?
Could pumping worsen another location?
Could pumping push contaminated water into a sensitive area?
Discharge Capacity
Can the receiving path accept the proposed flow?
Does the receiving path surcharge during storms?
Is the receiving water tide-locked or backflow-prone?
Is the downstream canal or drain already overloaded?
Are check valves or backflow controls needed?
Is discharge approval required?
Site Access
Can crews reach the site during or after flooding?
Is there safe maintenance access?
Can debris baskets be removed safely?
Can pumps be replaced safely?
Can sensors be cleaned safely?
Can the site be inspected without blocking traffic or evacuation?
Can emergency vehicles still pass?
Power
Is grid power available?
Is solar feasible?
Is there space for batteries?
Can batteries be placed above expected flood level?
Is generator support possible?
Is fuel-cell support realistic and permitted?
What critical loads must remain active?
What runtime is required?
Communications
Is cellular service available?
Is LoRa or local radio feasible?
Is wired backhaul available?
Is offline logging required?
Can data be retrieved manually if communications fail?
Who receives alerts?
Who reviews logs?
Maintenance
Who owns maintenance?
Who clears debris?
Who checks pumps?
Who cleans sensors?
Who replaces batteries?
Who reviews event logs?
Who responds to alerts?
Is maintenance funded?
Is a rainy-season inspection schedule possible?
Safety
Is the site exposed to traffic?
Is it exposed to pedestrians?
Are there open-water hazards?
Are there electrical hazards?
Is floodwater contaminated?
Are there trip hazards?
Are pump intakes protected?
Can the public access dangerous equipment?
Can equipment become debris during a flood?
Is vandalism likely?
Authority and Approval
Who owns the land?
Who owns the drainage asset?
Which public agency controls the site?
Is a permit required?
Is environmental review required?
Is road access approval required?
Is utility coordination required?
Is public consultation required?
Is emergency-management coordination required?
Site Readiness Levels
Site Readiness 0: Unknown

A site is at readiness level 0 if basic facts are unknown.

Unknowns may include:

site owner;
flood source;
discharge path;
maintenance owner;
safety constraints;
access constraints.

A level 0 site is not ready for design.

Site Readiness 1: Candidate

A site is a candidate if:

flooding is known or suspected;
public impact is plausible;
the location is accessible for review;
a possible discharge path exists;
a potential maintenance owner can be identified.

A candidate site is ready for preliminary review only.

Site Readiness 2: Reviewed Candidate

A site is a reviewed candidate if:

flood pain point is described;
water source is understood at a basic level;
discharge path is identified;
access constraints are noted;
power and communications options are noted;
maintenance owner is proposed;
safety issues are listed.

A reviewed candidate may be considered for proof-of-concept scoping.

Site Readiness 3: Pilot-Scoping Site

A site is ready for pilot scoping if:

responsible authority is engaged;
discharge path is under review;
maintenance ownership is realistic;
equipment location is identified;
baseline flood information is available or collectable;
safety review path is known;
validation metrics can be defined.

A level 3 site may be used to build a pilot plan.

Site Readiness 4: Approved Pilot Site

A site is an approved pilot site only if:

site access is approved;
discharge path is approved;
safety review is complete for the pilot scope;
maintenance owner is assigned;
power plan is approved;
communications plan is defined;
test plan is approved;
failure criteria are accepted;
public-works integration is defined.

A level 4 site may proceed to controlled pilot deployment if all required approvals are in place.

Site Readiness 5: Expansion Candidate

A site or group of sites becomes an expansion candidate only after measured pilot evidence exists.

Evidence may include:

measured flow;
water-level trends;
maintenance logs;
power runtime logs;
communications performance;
clog detection records;
operator feedback;
post-event review;
pass/fail evaluation.

A level 5 site may support multi-lot cluster planning or future Level 2 building review.

Strong Site Indicators

A strong HydroGrid Lot site has:

repeated flooding;
high social or operational importance;
bounded flood area;
valid discharge path;
maintainable intake location;
measurable water movement;
available power strategy;
communications path;
safe maintenance access;
clear responsible owner;
public-works relevance;
realistic success criteria.
Weak Site Indicators

A weak site has:

unknown water source;
unknown discharge path;
no maintenance owner;
no access during flooding;
high vandalism risk with no mitigation;
unsafe traffic exposure;
no way to measure improvement;
no public authority engagement;
flood caused mainly by basin-scale overflow beyond local control;
receiving water already too high during target events;
expectation of citywide flood elimination.

A weak site may still be important, but it is not a good first pilot.

Best First Pilot Site

The best first pilot site is not necessarily the worst flood location.

The best first pilot site is one where:

the flood pain point is real;
the system boundary is clear;
water source and destination are understandable;
equipment can be installed safely;
maintenance can happen;
measured flow is possible;
event logs can be reviewed;
the partner can compare before and after conditions;
success and failure can be judged honestly.

A pilot should be designed to learn, not to overpromise.

Manila-Oriented Site Logic

For Manila or Metro Manila use cases, candidate site categories may include:

low road segments that repeatedly pond;
underpasses;
evacuation-center access routes;
school access routes used during disasters;
clinic or hospital approach roads;
pump-station approaches;
canal-side trash chokepoints;
estuary or coastal outfall areas;
barangay roads where access is repeatedly lost;
locations where power instability disrupts flood response.

This guide does not name specific deployment sites.

Actual site selection must be performed with local data, local authority involvement, and qualified review.

HydroGrid Lot Placement Pattern

A HydroGrid Lot should usually be placed near the failure point, but not always directly inside the deepest water.

Useful placement factors include:

safe access;
proximity to intake;
proximity to discharge;
ability to protect equipment;
ability to route hoses or pipes;
ability to service debris baskets;
ability to power the system;
line of sight or communications access;
distance from public traffic hazards;
flood-safe elevation for controls and batteries.

The intake may be in the flood-prone low point while the control enclosure sits on higher ground.

Sensor Placement Pattern

Sensors should be placed to answer specific questions.

Water-Level Sensor

Purpose:

detect rising water;
measure flood depth;
support threshold alerts;
track flood duration.

Placement:

near the flood-prone point;
protected from direct debris impact;
accessible for cleaning;
referenced to a known local datum where possible.
Flow Meter

Purpose:

prove water moved;
measure flow rate;
totalize volume.

Placement:

on discharge line or approved measurement section;
installed according to manufacturer requirements;
accessible for inspection and calibration.
Pressure Sensor

Purpose:

detect outlet blockage;
detect abnormal pump conditions;
estimate hydraulic load.

Placement:

on discharge line or pump manifold where safe and useful.
Pump-Current Sensor

Purpose:

detect pump load;
support dry-run or clog detection;
support power logging.

Placement:

in the protected control enclosure or motor-control circuit.
Salinity Sensor

Purpose:

detect coastal or estuary backflow;
distinguish seawater influence from rainfall-only flooding.

Placement:

at relevant outfall, canal, or backflow-prone location;
protected from fouling and debris;
scheduled for cleaning.
Vibration Sensor

Purpose:

detect pump health issues;
detect cavitation or debris interaction;
support maintenance.

Placement:

on pump housing, motor mount, or structure where meaningful.
Pump Placement Pattern

Pumps should be placed with attention to:

priming;
intake depth;
debris exposure;
service access;
discharge head;
electrical safety;
check valve location;
backflow isolation;
vibration;
noise;
maintenance removal.

A pump that cannot be serviced quickly may become a liability.

Control Enclosure Placement

The control enclosure should be placed:

above expected flood level;
away from direct debris impact;
accessible to authorized maintenance;
protected from public tampering;
protected from rain and splash;
ventilated or thermally managed as required;
close enough to sensors and pumps for reliable wiring;
safely grounded and protected.

Battery systems should be located with additional fire, thermal, and electrical safety review.

Discharge Placement Pattern

Discharge routing must avoid:

pushing water into another flood-prone road;
undermining soil;
eroding embankments;
contaminating sensitive areas;
creating slip hazards;
blocking traffic;
creating uncontrolled spray;
causing backflow through the same system.

Discharge should be reviewed by qualified personnel.

Maintenance Access Pattern

A site must allow crews to:

inspect intake;
remove debris;
replace pump;
clean sensors;
read local status;
retrieve logs;
access control enclosure;
isolate power;
shut down the system safely;
perform post-event inspection.

If maintenance cannot safely occur, the site should not be selected.

Baseline Data Needed

Before a pilot, partners should collect baseline information where possible.

Useful baseline data includes:

dates of previous flooding;
approximate flood depth;
duration of flooding;
rainfall context;
known clogged drains;
maintenance history;
pump-station status if relevant;
traffic or access impact;
photos or official reports where available;
local observations;
existing drainage maps;
known discharge path.

The repository should not invent baseline data.

If baseline data is missing, the pilot may begin with monitoring before pump support.

Pilot Site Scoring

A simple scoring approach can help compare sites.

Score each category from 0 to 3.

```
0 = unknown or unacceptable
1 = weak
2 = workable
3 = strong
```

Categories:

repeated flood history;
public importance;
bounded system area;
discharge path clarity;
maintenance access;
equipment safety;
power feasibility;
communications feasibility;
measurement feasibility;
authority engagement;
debris manageability;
expansion value.

A high-scoring site is not automatically approved.

A low-scoring site may still be important, but it may not be a good first pilot.

Red Flags

Do not select a site if:

the system would discharge water into another vulnerable community;
public safety cannot be protected;
equipment would obstruct evacuation;
maintenance cannot happen;
electrical equipment would be submerged;
pumps would operate without flow measurement;
the site owner is unknown;
public authority approval is absent;
contaminated water handling is ignored;
the project is being presented as guaranteed flood protection;
the expected flood source is too large for local intervention;
there is no way to define success or failure.

A completed site-selection process should produce:

flood pain point summary;
affected people or access function;
water source description;
discharge path description;
maintenance owner;
safety concerns;
power plan;
communications plan;

recommendation.

This output should be stored as a real project file only when based on real site review.

Relationship to Level 2 Building Path

Some sites may eventually justify a HydroGrid Flood-Resilience Building.

This should be considered only if:

lot-scale support is insufficient;
the site has high public value;
repeated flood evidence exists;
discharge capacity exists or can be built;
public-works authority is engaged;
land is available;
permanent infrastructure is justified;
engineering and permitting resources exist;
long-term maintenance is funded.

The building path is a future option, not a first assumption.

Summary

Site selection determines whether IX-Bayanihan-HydroGrid has a chance to help.

A good site has a real flood pain point, a valid water destination, safe maintenance access, measurable outcomes, and responsible ownership.

A poor site can make even good equipment useless.

The first HydroGrid Lot should be placed where the system can learn honestly, measure clearly, and help a specific flood chokepoint without pretending to solve every flood around it.
